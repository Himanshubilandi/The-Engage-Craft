01

Overview of Data Communication & Networking

Data communication is simply the exchange of data between two devices — like your phone sending a message to your friend's phone. For this to work, you need five things:

  • A sender — the device sending data
  • A receiver — the device getting the data
  • A medium — the path data travels through (wire, cable, or air)
  • A message — the actual data (text, voice, video...)
  • A protocol — the agreed rules both sides follow
i

Imagine sending a letter: you (sender), the postal system (medium), your friend (receiver), the letter (message), and the shared language + envelope format (protocol). Miss any one piece, and communication fails.

A network is just a group of devices connected together so they can share data and resources — files, internet, printers.

  • Share information instantly (email, chat, files)
  • Share hardware (one printer used by many computers)
  • Save money (fewer devices needed per person)
  • Centralize data (one place to store and back everything up)

i

PAN = talking to the person next to you. LAN = talking to your whole classroom. MAN = talking to your whole city. WAN = talking to the whole world. The bigger the network, the slower and more complex it gets.

03

LAN Technologies

A LAN connects devices within a small area. Here are the main technologies used to build one:

  • Ethernet — the most common wired LAN technology; data travels in small chunks called frames over cables, at speeds from 10 Mbps up to 10+ Gbps
  • Wi-Fi (Wireless LAN) — same idea as Ethernet, but uses radio waves instead of cables, connecting through a wireless router/access point
  • Token Ring (older) — devices in a ring pass around a small packet called a "token"; only whoever holds it can send data, avoiding collisions
i

Ethernet is friends passing notes through fixed tubes (cables). Wi-Fi is the same group shouting through the air instead. Token Ring is passing a "talking stick" around a circle — only the holder can speak.

04

Topologies

Topology means the physical layout — how cables/connections are arranged between devices.

Bus — all devices share a single main cable.

Dev1 --- Dev2 --- Dev3 --- Dev4 (all share one central cable)

Star — all devices connect to one central switch/hub (most common today).

[Switch/Hub] / | | \ Dev1 Dev2 Dev3 Dev4

Ring — each device connects to exactly two neighbors, forming a closed loop.

Dev1 --- Dev2 | | Dev4 --- Dev3

Mesh — every device connects directly to every other device. Very reliable, but expensive and cable-heavy.

Tree — multiple star networks connected in a hierarchy, like branches of a tree.

Hybrid — a mix of two or more topologies; most real-world networks are actually this.

i

Bus = one shared hallway. Star = a central reception desk everyone talks through. Ring = people in a circle passing a message hand to hand. Mesh = everyone has everyone else's direct number. Tree = departments connected to a head office.

05

Models — The OSI Model

The OSI Model is a 7-layer conceptual framework explaining how data travels from one device to another, step by step. Each layer has one job, stacked like a sandwich.

7ApplicationWhere you interact — browsers, email apps
6PresentationFormats/encrypts/compresses data
5SessionOpens, manages, and closes the connection
4TransportSplits data into pieces, ensures reliable delivery
3NetworkChooses the best route (uses IP addresses)
2Data LinkDirect link between two connected devices (MAC addresses)
1PhysicalActual hardware — cables, signals, radio waves

Easy way to remember, top to bottom:

AllPeopleSeemToNeedDataProcessing
i

Mailing a gift: you pick the gift (Application), wrap it (Presentation), arrange a courier pickup (Session), split it into numbered parcels (Transport), the courier picks routes/trucks (Network), the local driver hands it to your neighbor's door (Data Link), and the actual road + truck move it physically (Physical). Each layer only does its own job and passes the result along.

06

The TCP/IP Stack

The TCP/IP Model is what the real Internet actually runs on today — just 4 layers, because it merges several OSI layers together.

4Application= OSI's App + Presentation + Session — your browser, email, file transfer
3TransportReliable (TCP) or fast-but-unchecked (UDP) delivery
2InternetAddressing & routing using IP addresses
1Network Access= OSI's Data Link + Physical — cables, Wi-Fi signals

TCP checks every piece of data arrives correctly and in order (web pages, emails, downloads). UDP is faster but doesn't guarantee delivery (video calls, gaming, streaming).

i

TCP is a registered letter — the post office confirms delivery, and resends a missing page. UDP is shouting across a room — fast, but if someone misses it, no one repeats it.
