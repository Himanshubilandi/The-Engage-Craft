01

Introduction to the Physical Layer

The Physical Layer is the very bottom layer of any network model (Layer 1 in OSI). Its only job is to move raw bits (0s and 1s) from one device to another as real physical signals — electrical pulses, light flashes, or radio waves.

  • Doesn't understand meaning, addresses, or errors — it just moves raw signals
  • Deals with cables, connectors, voltage levels, and signal timing
  • Everything above it (Data Link, Network...) depends on this layer working correctly
i

Think of the Physical Layer as the road itself — it doesn't care what's inside the truck driving on it (the data), it just makes sure there's a solid surface for the truck to move on.

02

Digital Transmission & Modes

Digital transmission means sending data as a series of 0s and 1s. But there's a choice to make: how many bits travel at once, and how the sender and receiver stay in sync.

Parallel vs Serial (how many bits travel together)

  • Parallel transmission — multiple bits travel side-by-side at the same time, on separate wires. Fast, but only works well over short distances.
  • Serial transmission — bits travel one after another, on a single wire. Slower per moment, but works reliably over long distances (used in most real networks, including the Internet).

Synchronous vs Asynchronous (how sender/receiver stay in sync)

  • Synchronous — data sent in a continuous stream, with sender and receiver sharing a timing clock. Efficient for large amounts of data.
  • Asynchronous — each small chunk of data (like one character) has a start bit and stop bit around it, so the receiver knows exactly where each piece begins and ends — no shared clock needed.
i

Parallel is several people carrying one box each side-by-side through a wide doorway. Serial is one person carrying boxes one at a time through a narrow doorway. Synchronous is a marching band keeping the same beat; asynchronous is people walking individually but each one shouting "starting now!" and "done!" so others know when they begin and finish.

03

Digital-to-Digital Conversion

This is about turning digital data (0s and 1s in a computer) into a digital signal that can travel over a cable. This is done through line coding — deciding what voltage/pulse pattern represents a 0 and what represents a 1.

Unipolar Polar (NRZ, RZ, Manchester) Bipolar
  • Unipolar — only one voltage level used (e.g., 0V for 0, +5V for 1)
  • Polar — uses both positive and negative voltages (e.g., +V for 1, −V for 0) — includes popular schemes like Manchester encoding
  • Bipolar — represents 0 as no signal, but alternates the voltage (+ and −) for consecutive 1s, which helps detect errors
i

Think of it like Morse code — you're deciding what pattern of "dots and dashes" (voltage pulses) will represent each bit, so the receiver can correctly read back the original 0s and 1s.

Analog-to-Digital Conversion

This converts an analog signal (like your voice, a continuous wave) into digital data (0s and 1s) — this is exactly what happens when you speak into a phone and it becomes digital data.

The most common method is PCM (Pulse Code Modulation), done in three steps:

1. Sampling
→
2. Quantization
→
3. Encoding
  • Sampling — measuring the analog wave's height at many points in time
  • Quantization — rounding each sample to the nearest allowed value
  • Encoding — converting each rounded value into binary (0s and 1s)
i

Imagine photographing a smoothly moving car every second (sampling), then rounding its position to the nearest mark on a ruler (quantization), then writing that number down in binary (encoding). Enough snapshots, and you can recreate the motion closely enough.

05

Analog Transmission

Analog transmission means sending data as a continuous wave (like a radio signal) instead of as clean digital pulses. It's needed when digital signals must travel long distances through the air or through mediums (like phone lines) that carry analog signals better.

To send data this way, we modulate a signal — meaning we take a steady "carrier wave" and change one of its properties (amplitude, frequency, or phase) to represent the data.

i

Think of a carrier wave like a steady humming note. To send a message, you subtly change its pitch, loudness, or timing — the listener on the other end can then decode those small changes back into the original message.

06

Digital-to-Analog Conversion

This converts digital data (0s and 1s) into an analog signal, so it can travel over analog-friendly mediums like radio or telephone lines. There are three basic techniques:

TechniqueWhat changesIdea
ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying)Wave height1 = strong wave, 0 = weak/no wave
FSK (Frequency Shift Keying)Wave frequency1 = one frequency, 0 = another frequency
PSK (Phase Shift Keying)Wave phase (timing shift)1 and 0 represented by shifting where the wave "starts"
i

Imagine sending Morse code using a torch: ASK is flashing it bright vs dim, FSK is blinking fast vs slow, PSK is starting the blink slightly earlier or later than expected. Different tricks, same goal — encode 0s and 1s onto a wave.

07

Analog-to-Analog Conversion

This converts one analog signal into another analog signal, ready for transmission — this is exactly how radio broadcasting works.

TechniqueWhat changesEveryday example
AM (Amplitude Modulation)Wave height varies with the signalAM radio
FM (Frequency Modulation)Wave frequency varies with the signalFM radio (clearer sound)
PM (Phase Modulation)Wave phase shifts with the signalUsed in some digital radio systems
i

AM radio is like adjusting how loud your voice gets to carry meaning. FM radio is like changing your pitch instead — which is why FM usually sounds clearer, since background noise affects loudness more than pitch.

08

Transmission Media

Transmission media is the actual physical path data travels through — the "road" itself. These are called guided media because the signal is guided through a solid, physical path.

Twisted-Pair Cable Coaxial Cable Fiber Optic Cable
  • Twisted-pair cable — two insulated copper wires twisted together (twisting reduces interference); cheap, used in home/office LANs and telephone lines
  • Coaxial cable — a central copper wire surrounded by insulation and a metal shield; used for cable TV and older networks, handles more data than twisted-pair
  • Fiber optic cable — sends data as pulses of light through a thin glass/plastic strand; extremely fast, long-distance, and immune to electrical interference — used for the internet's backbone
i

Twisted-pair is a small local street. Coaxial is a slightly wider avenue with a protective barrier. Fiber optic is a high-speed superhighway made entirely of light instead of cars.

09

Wireless Transmission

Unlike guided media, wireless transmission sends signals through open air — no physical cable needed. This is called unguided media.

  • Radio waves — travel long distances, pass through walls; used for Wi-Fi, FM radio, and mobile networks
  • Microwaves — travel in a straight line and need line-of-sight (no obstacles); used for satellite communication and long-distance point-to-point links
  • Infrared — short range, cannot pass through walls; used in TV remotes and short-range device communication
i

Radio waves are like shouting — they spread everywhere and pass through obstacles. Microwaves are like using a laser pointer — you need a clear, direct line of sight. Infrared is like a flashlight beam — very short range and blocked instantly by a wall.

Switching Techniques

When many devices share the same network, we need a way to decide how data actually finds its path from sender to receiver. That's what switching handles.

Circuit Switching

A dedicated path is set up between sender and receiver before data transfer begins, and stays reserved for the entire conversation — like a traditional telephone call.

Data is broken into small packets, and each packet can travel independently, possibly via different paths, then gets reassembled at the destination. This is how the internet actually works.

Circuit switching is booking a private taxi for the whole trip, exclusively yours. Packet switching is splitting your luggage into several parcels and letting each one find its own courier route, meeting you at the destination. Message switching is handing your entire suitcase to one post office, which fully stores it before sending it whole to the next post office down the line.
